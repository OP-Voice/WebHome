A Measured, Low-Cost Solution

Proposal: Housing Advisory Commission

  • ✓
    Advisory Role: To study housing issues and provide informed, balanced recommendations to the City Council.
  • ✓
    Inclusive Representation: At least one-third of seats reserved for renters, ensuring their perspective is heard.
  • ✓
    Collaborative Focus: A forum for renters, homeowners, developers, and the city to find common ground.

Addressing Potential Concerns

Myth: "It's just more cost and bureaucracy."

Fact: It's an investment in smarter governance. This is a low-cost, high-impact tool for developing more effective and equitable policies that reflect the entire community, preventing more costly housing crises down the road.

Myth: "Existing channels are sufficient."

Fact: Existing channels were not designed to represent renters. While informal feedback is possible, it is no substitute for a formal, structured seat at the table where the unique challenges of 31,000 households can be consistently addressed.

Myth: "This is a backdoor to rent control."

Fact: The commission's role is strictly advisory, not regulatory. The goal is to foster collaboration between all stakeholders—renters, landlords, and developers—to find balanced solutions that ensure market stability, not to impose price controls.

Myth: "This isn't the city's role."

Fact: Housing is infrastructure. A stable housing market is essential for economic development, workforce-retention, and community health—all core city responsibilities. The city's own data confirms a housing gap; this is a proactive step to address it.

Myth: "Just add renters to an existing committee."

Fact: The NEC is designed for homeowner issues like zoning and property upkeep. Housing affordability is a complex, city-wide economic issue that requires its own dedicated focus to ensure the voices of renters, landlords, and housing experts are central to the conversation, not a footnote.

Myth: "Renters don't pay their fair share for a voice."

Fact: Renters already pay property taxes. They are a major operating cost for landlords and are factored directly into the price of rent. Every month, 31,000 households contribute to the city's tax base. Their financial contribution is present; their formal representation is not.
